4. D I S C U S S I O N
       As can be seen in Table III, the results obtained from the
Dcbye-Htickel equation when c = 0, do not differ appreciably from
those obtained when c ;e 0 is considered. The same comment can be
made when looking at the results obtained from the Pitzer equation
which are summarized in Table IV, although in this case, an important
discrepancy of 0.005 units in (?e)a appears at 70 weight % of ethanol.
So, we can conclude that the extended Debye-Hiickel equation and the
Pitzer equation are valid for fitting the results obtained for NaC1 up to
70 weight % of ethanol.
       The values obtained for the parameters of the Pitzer equation in
mixtures with 80 and 90 weight % of ethanol, appear extremely high
and they lack physical meaning. The explanation of such high values
must be looked for in the appearance of ionic association in these media
as a consequence of their low dielectric constant. This ionic association
causes an important drop in the value of the concentration of free ions
present in solution (from m to c~m) which must be taken into considera-
tion. Although the Pitzer equation can compensate for some of this
effect, such a compensation is not enough to provide meaningful values.
The introduction of the chemical model avoids all these problems and
leads to very good results. As can be seen in Table V, the ionic associa-
tion appears at 60 weight % of ethanol, being marked when the ethanol
content reaches 80 and 90 weight % (large values of the association
constants).
